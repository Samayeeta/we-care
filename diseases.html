Mental Illnesses faced by advanced cancer patients

Both patients and caregivers are distressed by advanced cancer (s). Patients must cope with the mental effects of their disease and poor prognosis in addition to enduring physical debility. While the majority of people are able to overcome these obstacles, others do not. Listed below are a few illnesses cancer patients may acquire with time:

Adjustment Disorder

What is it?

Emotional and/or behavioural symptoms that are more severe than would be expected as a result of exposure to a particular stressor. Adjustment disorder is described as distressing symptoms of situational anxiety or depression that are severe enough to require treatment but not severe enough to meet the requirements for a complete anxiety or depressive disorder. Insomnia, worry, muscle tension, restlessness, mild shortness of breath, palpitations, sweating, trembling or shaking (“the jitters”), and feeling dizzy or lightheaded are all symptoms of situational anxiety.

Irritability, mood swings, tearfulness, poor concentration, social isolation, and transient bouts of hopelessness or demoralisation are all symptoms of situational depression. Both types of symptoms can lead to a greater need for emotional support from caregivers.

Assessment

Anxiety, depression, or both may be signs of adjustment disorders. The oncologist should ask about the above signs, as well as changes in symptom severity, actions at home and at work, how the patient is coping, and any cancer-related issues. Concerns about employment, finances, parenting, marital and family relationships, loss of freedom, sexual function, and end-of-life problems, as well as pain and worry about changing tumour markers, may all contribute to psychological distress. Cathartic weeping indicates an adjustment disorder, while depressive crying is exhausting and unrelieving. The Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS) is a short, self-report instrument that can be used to measure and track discomfort in patients with advanced illness, despite the fact that there is no suitable questionnaire for diagnosing adjustment disorders in cancer patients. An adjustment disorder is indicated by a cumulative score of 10 to 11, while severe depression is indicated by a score of 19 to 20.

Treatment

-Adjustment disorders are treated with a compassionate, problem-focused approach.

-Benzodiazepines can help with anxiety and insomnia.

-Trazodone is a non-habit forming, healthy, and reliable sleep aid.-When patients bring up or allude to questions about loved ones, meaning, future treatment choices, and end-of-life problems, oncologists should listen for and facilitate discussion of these issues. The use of a multidisciplinary team and telephone monitoring has been shown to minimise symptoms of psychological distress in patients with advanced disease.Successful care is supported by strong coordination and communication with mental health professionals about medical conditions, prognosis, and patient concerns.

-SSRI stands for selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor; SNRI stands for serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors; NMS stands for neuroleptic malignant syndrome. Every day, qD; HS, before going to bed; qHS, every night; TID, three times a day; BID, twice a day EPS stands for extrapyramidal signs, and QTc stands for corrected QT interval on an electrocardiogram.

-Supportive expressive therapy focuses on current life issues, important relationships, barriers to successful coping, and emotional communication in a responsive environment.

-Patients in cognitive behaviour therapy (CBT) are advised to recognise excessively negative or irrational interpretations (cognitions) of themselves, their condition, and their life situation; examine how these feelings are converted into unhealthy actions; and then be coached to adopt more realistic cognitions and functional behaviours. CBT may also provide stress management instruction in the form of relaxation techniques. Both CBT and positive expressive therapy have been shown to alleviate anxiety and pain symptoms in advanced cancer patients.
